What is cardiovascular disease?

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is a group of heart and blood vessel conditions. These include problems like heart attacks, stroke, and artery blockages. CVD is often caused by things like high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, and being overweight. It's a major cause of illness and death worldwide, but many cases can be prevented by living a healthy lifestyle, like eating well, staying active, and not smoking. Taking care of these things can lower your chances of getting heart disease.

How about the level of awareness among the public toward cardiovascular disease?

These days, there is a decline in public awareness of this disease’s dangers, and to make matters worse, some people are even unaware that it even exists.

Why we need to give attention to cardiovascular disease?

It's important to pay attention to cardiovascular disease (CVD) because it's a leading cause of illness and death worldwide and we can have serious consequences for health and quality of life.

What can we do to prevent cardiovascular disease?

Preventing cardiovascular disease (CVD) involves adopting a healthy lifestyle. This means eating a balanced diet, staying physically active, not smoking, and limiting alcohol intake.

Cardiovascular Disease Types

Dive into detailed insights on various types of cardiovascular diseases. Gain a better understanding of the symptoms, causes, and treatments specific to each condition, empowering yourself with knowledge to safeguard your heart health

  • Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)
  • Heart Failure
  • Stroke
  • Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD)
  • Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

Death Rate: Approximately 610,000 deaths annually (Centre for Disease Control & Prevention).

Coronary artery disease (CAD) develops when the blood vessels that deliver oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle become narrowed or blocked, often due to the accumulation of plaque. This condition can result in symptoms such as chest pain (angina), heart attacks, and various other complications, highlighting the critical need for proactive measures to manage and prevent its progression.

Heart Failure

Death Rate: About 35% will survive for 10 years (Medical News Today).

Heart failure occurs when the heart muscle becomes weakened or stiffened, hindering its ability to effectively pump blood throughout the body. This can manifest in symptoms like shortness of breath, fatigue, and swelling in the legs and ankles, necessitating prompt medical intervention and lifestyle adjustments to manage the condition effectively.

Stroke

Death Rate: Annually, 15 million people worldwide suffer a stroke. Of these, 5 million die and another 5 million are left permanently disabled (World Health Organization).

A stroke occurs when the blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted or reduced, depriving brain tissue of oxygen and nutrients. This can cause brain damage and lead to symptoms such as sudden numbness or weakness in the face, arm, or leg, confusion, trouble speaking or understanding speech, and difficulty walking.

Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD)

Death Rate: PAD accounted for 0.13% of total deaths (0.074–0.23%) globally (National Institute of Health).

PAD is a condition in which the blood vessels outside of the heart, usually in the legs, become narrowed or blocked, restricting blood flow. This can result in symptoms such as leg pain or cramping during activity (claudication), numbness or weakness in the legs, and slow-healing wounds or ulcers on the feet.

Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)

Death Rate: The condition contributes to about 158,000 deaths each year (National Institute of Health).

AFib is a type of irregular heartbeat (arrhythmia) characterized by rapid, chaotic electrical signals in the heart's upper chambers (atria). This can lead to symptoms such as palpitations, fatigue, dizziness, and shortness of breath, and increases the risk of stroke.

Cardiovascular Disease Factors

Explore key factors influencing cardiovascular health and disease risk. Learn about lifestyle habits, genetics, and environmental influences affecting your heart health. Empower yourself with knowledge to make heart-healthy choices

Smoking/Tobacco use

Smoking damages the blood vessels, reduces oxygen in the blood, and increases the buildup of plaque in the arteries. It is a major risk factor for heart disease, stroke, and other cardiovascular conditions.

High Blood Pressure

High blood pressure, or hypertension, occurs when the force of blood against the artery walls is consistently too high. This can damage arteries and increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, and other complications.

Unhealthy Diet

A diet high in saturated and trans fats, cholesterol, sodium, and sugar increases the risk of developing high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and obesity, all of which are major risk factors for cardiovascular disease.

Physical Inactivity

Lack of regular physical activity is associated with an increased risk of obesity, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and diabetes. Engaging in regular exercise can help lower these risk factors and improve heart health.

High Cholesterol Levels

High cholesterol levels, particularly high levels of LDL ("bad") cholesterol, can lead to the buildup of plaque in the arteries, narrowing them and restricting blood flow. This increases the risk of heart disease and stroke.

Family History and Genetics

A family history of cardiovascular disease, as well as genetic factors, can increase an individual's risk of developing heart disease. These factors may include genetic conditions such as familial hypercholesterolemia or inherited heart defects.
